The ship

Explora I is a small luxury ship, around 900 guests, so it stays quiet. Every room is a suite with an ocean view and a private terrace. It has a doctor, laundry, a spa, and a pool on board.

One price, no add-ons

The fare covers all meals across nine restaurants, all drinks, wifi, and tips. The shuttle into each town comes with it. Nothing gets added to a bill during the week.

The daily rhythm

You unpack once. The ship moves at night, so you wake up somewhere new each morning. Every day you decide to take a guided tour in port or stay aboard. You have dinner together each night with no reservation to chase.

Why it fits the two of you

No long drives and no dragging suitcases between hotels. You see five places in a week at a steady pace. It suits the two of you, covering a lot of ground without rushing.

DAY 03Sat · Aug 1Montserrat and the summit hike
MONTSERRAT · 41.6°N 1.83°E · 1,236 m

You spend the day on a serrated mountain an hour outside the city, at a Benedictine monastery wedged into the rock since the Middle Ages. There is a real hike up top, the venerated Black Madonna down in the basilica, and a horizon that reaches the Pyrenees on a clear day. Bring a layer; the summit runs cooler and windier than the city.
Getting thereTake the FGC R5 from Plaça d'Espanya, about an hour to the mountain base, then choose either the rack railway or the Aeri cable car. They are alternative final legs from different stations. The Trans Montserrat pass (€43.80) covers the metro, train, your chosen ascent, and the funiculars.
The hikeRide the Sant Joan funicular to the upper station, then walk the ridge to the Sant Jeroni summit, the highest point at 1,236 m, for a full turn of the horizon. For a longer day, skip the funicular and climb the whole loop from the monastery, about 10.5 km over four hours.
At the monasteryThe basilica holds La Moreneta, the black Madonna carved in wood; you file past her in a side chapel (monastery ticket €18, shoulders and knees covered). The boys' choir that usually sings here is on summer break until around Aug 20.
BringTwo to three liters of water, a hat, sunscreen, and a windbreaker for the wind up top.

DAY 04Sun · Aug 2Mass at Sagrada, then Gaudí
SAGRADA FAMÍLIA · 41.40°N 2.17°E

You start the morning inside Gaudí's basilica during the free Sunday Mass, when the forest of stone columns fills with colored light and the space is used for what it was built for. The rest of the day is the city's Gaudí greatest hits: the mosaics of Park Güell and, if you book it, a climb up one of the Sagrada's towers.

You sit in the nave for the service, and the stained glass at that hour is the whole point. Check the basilica is open first, since a centenary-year works notice went up.
Park GüellBook a slot around 11:30 to 12:00 for the monumental zone (€18, timed entry only): the terrace with the wave-shaped tiled bench and the mosaic lizard.
AfternoonBook a separate Sagrada tour-and-tower ticket (€36) to ride the elevator up a spire and look down the length of the nave. For a quieter service, Barcelona Cathedral has Mass at 10:30 and Santa Maria del Mar an international Mass at noon.

DAY 08Thu · Aug 6Carthage and Sidi Bou Said
TUNIS · LA GOULETTE · 36.8°N 10.3°E

This is the day the trip goes somewhere genuinely far from home. You dock in Tunisia and drive out to Carthage, the Phoenician and later Roman city that Rome spent three wars destroying, now a spread of ruins above the sea. Then up to Sidi Bou Said, a clifftop village painted in blue and white, for mint tea on a terrace over the water.

Sidi Bou SaidThe village is cobalt doors, white walls, and bougainvillea along a ridge. Have mint tea with pine nuts at a café terrace above the marina and browse the small shops.

DAY 09Fri · Aug 7Valletta and Mdina, Malta
VALLETTA · 35.9°N 14.5°E

Malta is a small, sun-baked island with an outsized history, built by the Knights of St. John. You explore Valletta, the honey-colored fortress capital, step into a cathedral that holds a Caravaggio, and drive up to Mdina, the old walled town so quiet they call it the Silent City.
VallettaThe capital is compact and walkable straight off the ship, a grid of golden limestone streets that drop toward the Grand Harbour. St. John's Co-Cathedral looks plain outside and is pure baroque gold within, and it holds Caravaggio's Beheading of St. John, his largest canvas.
MdinaRide up to Mdina, the medieval walled town on a hill in the island's center. Cars are mostly banned, the lanes are silent, and the bastion walls look out over half of Malta.
A quiet minuteBoth the co-cathedral and Mdina's cathedral are working churches, and empty enough for a few minutes on your own.

DAY 10Sat · Aug 8Taormina and Mount Etna
TAORMINA · 37.85°N 15.29°E

You tender into Sicily below Taormina, a cliff town wrapped around an ancient Greek theater with Mount Etna smoking in the distance. The volcano is the day's headline: a 4x4 up the black lava slopes and a walk along a crater rim, Europe's largest active volcano under your boots.
EtnaBook the ship's excursion or Etna People. You ride to around 1,900 m, then walk the rim of an old crater across cinder and lava, with the coast spread out below. Wear closed shoes and bring a layer; it is cool and windy at altitude even in August.
TaorminaThe Teatro Antico is a Greek theater the Romans rebuilt, and from the top row Etna frames the stage. The town is a stack of terraces and cafés, with a small beach at Isola Bella below.

DAY 13Tue · Aug 11Ancient Rome
ROMA · PALATINO · 41.89°N 12.49°E

You give the morning to the Romans. A timed ticket gets you into the Colosseum, then you walk it off through the Forum and up the Palatine, the hill where the emperors lived, with the whole ancient city laid out below. The afternoon is the rest of the centro: the Pantheon, a couple of piazzas, and the Trevi Fountain.
The ancient coreBook the combined Colosseum, Forum, and Palatine ticket (€18, good 24 hours) on colosseo.it. The Colosseum needs a timed slot, and the arena-floor and underground add-ons sell out first, so grab them early. Go at opening before the heat.
AfternoonWalk to the Pantheon, still roofed by its 2,000-year-old dome with the open eye at the top, then Piazza Navona and the Trevi Fountain. In 2026, entry to Trevi's lower basin costs €2 from 9:00 to 22:00. The view from the upper piazza and access after hours stay free. Refill your bottle at the nasoni, the little iron street fountains that run cold all over the city.

DAY 14Wed · Aug 12The Vatican
CITTÀ DEL VATICANO · 41.90°N 12.45°E

You go early to beat both the heat and the crowds. A guided early entry walks you through the Vatican Museums to the Sistine Chapel before the public doors open, then out into St. Peter's, where you can climb the dome for a view straight down the nave and out over the square.
Museums and SistineBook an early-entry guided tour with Walks of Italy (about $60 to $85), or buy Vatican Museums self-entry for €20 plus the €5 online booking, €25 total, and reserve weeks ahead. The galleries build for a mile toward the one room everyone comes for; getting there early makes the visit calmer.
St. Peter's and the domeThe basilica is free to enter. The dome climb is €17 by stairs or €22 by lift, followed by steps, and the reward is the view down onto the altar and out across the colonnade.
Under the churchIf you book it ahead, the Scavi tour takes a small group beneath the basilica to the necropolis and what is held to be St. Peter's tomb, one of the quietest and most affecting hours in Rome.
The PopeThe reigning pope is Leo XIV, and he is in Rome this week. The official Vatican calendar lists a general audience on Wed Aug 12 at 10:00 and the Angelus on Sun Aug 16 at noon in St. Peter's Square. Reconfirm both close to travel. The Aug 12 audience competes with the early Museums plan, so pick one.

DAY 16Fri · Aug 14The coastal hike
SENTIERO AZZURRO · 44.13°N 9.68°E

The big walking day. You follow the old footpath that links the villages along the cliffs, Monterosso to Vernazza to Corniglia, up through vineyards and down into harbors, 5 to 6 hours in August with the heat, queues, and stone stairs. You start early, before the heat and the crowds arrive.
The trailThe Blue Path from Monterosso to Vernazza to Corniglia is open in 2026. Allow 5 to 6 hours in August with the heat, queues, and stone stairs. Vernazza, halfway, is the prettiest of the five, so break there for coffee and a swim in the little harbor. The low Corniglia-to-Manarola stretch is closed for landslide repairs until around 2028. The walking alternative is the higher Corniglia to Volastra to Manarola route, which is longer, steeper, and exposed, or take the train. Check the park's live status before setting out.
Do it wellSet off around 7:00, wear real shoes (they turn back people in flip-flops), and carry 1.5 to 2 liters of water. End the day at Manarola for the sunset from the rocks below the village, with a glass at the Nessun Dorma wine bar.
With companyFor someone to walk with, book a small-group guided coastal or Sanctuary-trail hike with Arbaspàa. It runs about $50 to $90 and takes the navigation off your hands.

DAY 17Sat · Aug 15Ferragosto, the day to plan around
VIA DELL'AMORE · 44.10°N 9.73°E

August 15 is Ferragosto, Italy's biggest summer holiday, and the whole country goes to the coast. The villages are packed and the trains fill, so you plan around it: an early booked slot on the easy Via dell'Amore, a climb to the quiet high trails, or a boat day on the water. In the evening, the Assumption Mass in a village church is worth catching wherever you are.
Beat the crowdsBook a timed slot on the Via dell'Amore, the short paved path between Manarola and Riomaggiore, first thing. Or climb above the crowds on a Sanctuary trail to the hilltop shrines, where almost no one goes. Or skip the trains and take a boat between the villages.
The holidayEverything is fuller and pricier today, so lock trains and any transfer ahead. The Assumption is the reason for the holiday, and the Mass at Monterosso's San Giovanni Battista or Vernazza's seafront Santa Margherita is a good place to be for it.
TonightTake an evening train to a Milan or Malpensa hotel tonight so tomorrow morning's flight stays safe. Aug 15 is your last Cinque Terre day.

Traveling alone

The solo playbook

The habits that make two weeks alone work, and the few ways solo travelers get burned.

Eating alone

Make lunch the big meal. Spain's menú del día and Italy's pranzo cost less, and a table for one at 1pm draws no attention. Sit at the bar or counter when you can: tapas bars, cicchetti counters, pizza al taglio. In Italy, aperitivo hour from 6 to 8pm often comes with a buffet, which makes a cheap, social light dinner. Book one food tour and one class per city early for built-in company.

Not getting pickpocketed

Barcelona and Rome rank among the worst cities for tourist theft. The hotspots are La Rambla, the Boqueria, the Sagrada queue, and Metro L3 in Barcelona; Termini, the Trevi Fountain, the Colosseum, and bus 64 in Rome. Your assault risk is low, but you make a prime target for distraction theft when you travel alone and look at a map. Wear a zipped crossbody in front, keep your wallet in a front pocket, never put your phone on the table, and split your cash and cards.

Beating the August heat

August can reach the high 90s Fahrenheit. Do the hard sightseeing at opening: towers, the Vatican, the Colosseum, and hikes. Slow down or go indoors midday, then start again after 5pm. Carry a refillable bottle, since Rome's nasoni fountains are free and everywhere, plus electrolytes, a hat, and sunscreen. Ferragosto on Aug 15 packs the trains and raises prices, so book the 15th and 16th ahead.

A faith rhythm to travel by

A Sunday Mass gives a solo week a fixed point. The 9am international Mass at Sagrada Família and Sunday Mass ashore in Sorrento are worth arriving early for. The Assumption Mass falls on Saturday in the Cinque Terre, and Sunday Aug 16 is a travel day. To see the Pope, choose the Aug 12 general audience during the Rome days or restructure the end for the Aug 16 Angelus in St. Peter's Square.
